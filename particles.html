The particles that glue
Japanese together

助詞 ・ じょし

Particles (助詞 / joshi) are the joints of a sentence. Japanese word order is loose because each little hiragana tag tells you the job a word is doing — who acts, what gets acted on, where, when, and how clauses connect. Learn the tag, and the sentence falls into place. Below: every common particle, grouped by job and colour, each with a memory hook and a tiny example. The but / because / and joiners and the like / seems family get their own expanded sections.

A

The big markers

who · what · where · when

These six do the heavy lifting in almost every sentence. They tag the topic, the doer, the object, and the setting. Master these first — everything else hangs off them.

は
wa say "wa", not "ha"
Topic — "as for ___"

Hook: the spotlight. It lifts something onto the stage; the rest of the sentence comments on it.

私は学生です。As for me, I'm a student.
が
ga
Subject — "it's THIS one"

Hook: the pointing finger. Introduces new info or picks out exactly which one. Question words always take が.

誰が来た？Who came? (which one, specifically)
を
o say "o"
Direct object

Hook: you only ever meet を in front of a verb. See を → "the verb hits this thing."

パンを食べる。Eat bread. (bread = the thing eaten)
に
ni
Point: time · place · target

Hook: the pin on the map. A precise point something arrives at, exists at, or is given to.

7時に東京に着く。Arrive in Tokyo at 7.
へ
e say "e"
Direction — "toward"

Hook: the glyph literally looks like an arrowhead → it points the way. Softer/vaguer than に.

東京へ行く。Head toward Tokyo.
で
de
Scene of action · by means of

Hook: "the scene & the tool." Where an action happens, or what you do it with.

電車で図書館で勉強する。Study at the library, by train.
B

Connectors

and · with · of · from · until

These link words to other words — possession, lists, ranges. Small but constant.

の
no
's / of (linking nouns)

Hook: the apostrophe-S. Glues nouns into a chain of belonging.

私の本my book (lit. me-'s book)
と
to
and (full list) · with · quote

Hook: "tied together." A complete list of exactly these, or doing it together with someone.

犬と猫a dog and a cat (just those two)
や
ya
and… and so on

Hook: "and stuff like." A partial list — these among others.

りんごやバナナapples, bananas, etc.
から
kara
from (start point)

Hook: the source. Where something begins. (Also "because" — see section D.)

9時からfrom 9 o'clock
まで
made
until / up to / as far as

Hook: the finish line. Pairs naturally with から.

駅まで歩く。Walk as far as the station.
C

Focus & limit

also · only · even

These add nuance — "too", "just", "even" — and often replace は / が / を rather than stacking on top of them.

も
mo
also / too / even

Hook: "me too" piggyback. Adds something to what's already there.

私も行く。I'll go too.
だけ
dake
only / just (neutral)

Hook: "just the one." Plain, no negative needed.

水だけjust water
しか
shika needs a NOT
nothing but ___

Hook: しか…ない. Always paired with a negative verb — "nothing but."

水しかない。There's nothing but water.
さえ
sae
even (extreme case)

Hook: "even THIS." Pushes to the surprising edge.

水さえない。There isn't even water.
ばかり
bakari
nothing but / always doing

Hook: "constantly." A bit exasperated — does nothing else.

食べてばかり。Does nothing but eat.
くらい
kurai / gurai
about / roughly

Hook: the "~ish" of amounts. Approximation.

10分くらいabout 10 minutes
こそ
koso
precisely / it's exactly ___

Hook: the underline. Emphasises "this one and no other."

今こそnow's the time
でも
demo
…or something / even

Hook: a casual suggestion — "coffee or something?"

コーヒーでもどう？How about coffee or something?
D

But · because · and

接続助詞 — the clause joiners

This is where most learners get stuck, because English uses one word ("but", "because") where Japanese has several — each with a different flavour. These all join two clauses inside one sentence (they sit at the end of the first clause). Here's how to tell them apart.

"But" — が ・ けど ・ のに

が / けど — plain "but"
高いけど、買う。
It's pricey, but I'll buy it.
のに — "even though…!"
勉強したのに、落ちた。
Even though I studied, I failed.
Tell them apart: が (formal) and けど (casual) are neutral "but." のに carries surprise or complaint — the result was the opposite of what you'd expect. If you feel frustration or "and yet!", reach for のに.

"Because" — から ・ ので

から — personal, blunt
寒いから、閉めて。
It's cold, so close it.
ので — soft, polite
遅れたので、すみません。
I'm sorry, as I was late.
Tell them apart: both mean "because." から is subjective and direct — your own reasoning, can sound assertive ("because I said so"). ので is softer and more objective — use it for polite excuses and requests.
し
shi
and what's more (stacking reasons)

Hook: "…and besides." Piles up reasons or qualities.

安いし、おいしい。It's cheap, and tasty too.
て
-te / -de
and then / and so (verb glue)

Hook: the universal connector. Chains actions in sequence.

起きて、食べて、行く。Get up, eat, and go.
ながら
nagara
while doing (same person)

Hook: "meanwhile, me." Two actions at once by one person.

歌いながら歩く。Walk while singing.
たり
-tari
doing things like A and B

Hook: a sample list of actions — "things such as…"

食べたり飲んだりする。Eat and drink (among other things).

The "if / when" joiners — と ・ ば ・ たら ・ なら

と — automatic result
押すと開く。
If/when you push it, it opens.
ば — general hypothetical
安ければ買う。
If it's cheap, I'll buy it.
たら — once A, then B
着いたら電話して。
Call me once you arrive.
なら — if it's the case that
行くなら京都へ。
If you're going, go to Kyoto.
Quick sort: と = unavoidable/natural consequence. ば = textbook "if". たら = the all-purpose spoken "if/when" (when in doubt, use this). なら = "if that's the situation you're describing."
E

Sentence enders

終助詞 — tone & attitude

These sit at the very end and colour the whole sentence with feeling — question, agreement, emphasis, doubt. They don't change meaning so much as mood.

か
ka
question

Hook: the spoken question mark.

行きますか？Are you going?
ね
ne
right? / isn't it?

Hook: "ne?" — nudging the listener to agree.

いいですね。That's nice, isn't it?
よ
yo
I'm telling you / heads up

Hook: "yo!" — new info the listener doesn't know yet.

もう始まるよ。It's starting, you know.
な
na
feeling… or "don't!"

Hook: two uses — soft musing (いいな = "ahh, nice"), but after a dictionary-form verb it's a sharp prohibition.

行くな。Don't go.
の
no
explaining / soft question

Hook: casual "…you see" or "what's up?" Common in speech.

どうしたの？What's wrong?
かな
kana
I wonder…

Hook: か (question) + な (musing) = wondering to yourself.

雨かな。I wonder if it'll rain.
F

Like & seems

みたい ・ よう ・ らしい ・ っぽい ・ そう

English "like" and "seems" cover comparison and guessing, so these five overlap and get confused. The trick is what each is based on — your eyes, your ears, or a resemblance — and how formal it is.

みたい
mitai
like / seems (casual)

Hook: the everyday "like." Resemblance or a casual guess.

子供みたい。(They're) like a child.
よう
yō (da / ni)
like / as if (formal twin of みたい)

Hook: formal みたい. Also ように = "so that / in such a way."

夢のようだ。It's like a dream.
らしい
rashii
apparently / so very ___

Hook: two jobs — "by all accounts" (hearsay), OR "the very essence of" a noun.

男らしい。Manly / very much a man.
っぽい
-ppoi
-ish / prone to

Hook: literally English "-ish," often a touch judgy.

子供っぽい。Childish.
そう
sō
looks like… / I hear that… (two meanings!)

The big trap: そう means two different things depending on what it attaches to — your eyes vs your ears.

Stem + そう = your eyes
おいしそう。
It looks tasty. (appearance)
Plain form + そう = your ears
おいしいそう。
I hear it's tasty. (hearsay)

Memory line: chop the word short (stem) → it's what you see. Leave it whole (plain form) → it's what you were told.

G

Sentence starters

接続詞 — conjunctions

Technically these aren't particles — they're conjunctions that begin a new sentence rather than joining clauses inside one. But since they're the standalone versions of "but / so / and", they belong on the same map.

でも / しかし
But / However

でも casual, しかし formal. Start of a contrasting sentence.

疲れた。でも行く。I'm tired. But I'll go.
だから / それで
So / Therefore

だから = "that's why"; それで = "and so then."

雨だ。だから休む。It's raining. So I'll rest.
なぜなら
Because (formal)

Sets up a reason, usually closed by …から. Written/formal.

なぜなら…から。The reason is… because…
そして / それから
And then

Sequencing two sentences — "and then next…"

食べた。そして寝た。I ate. And then I slept.
それに / さらに
Moreover / Furthermore

Adds another point on top — "what's more."

安い。それに近い。It's cheap. Plus it's close.
つまり / たとえば
In other words / For example

つまり restates; たとえば illustrates.

たとえば、寿司。For example, sushi.

Confusion-busters

the pairs everyone mixes up

If you only memorise one section, make it this one. These six contrasts cause the most mistakes.

は vs が — topic vs subject

は — known / the theme
私は学生です。
As for me, I'm a student. (here's the topic)
が — new / picks one out
誰が学生？
Who is the student? (which one?)
Fast test: question words (誰, 何, どれ) always take が, and so does the answer to them. Old, already-known info that sets the scene takes は. Inside relative clauses, use が.

に vs で — being vs doing

に — where it exists
公園にいる。
I'm at the park. (just existing there)
で — where action happens
公園で遊ぶ。
I play at the park. (an action occurs)
Rule of thumb: if a verb of doing something is involved, the location takes で. If it's about existing / arriving / being placed, use に.

から vs ので — blunt vs polite "because"

から = your personal reasoning, can sound forceful. ので = softer, more objective, the polite choice for excuses and requests. Same meaning, different temperature.

けど vs のに — neutral vs frustrated "but"

けど = plain contrast, no emotion. のに = the result defied expectations; carries surprise, regret, or complaint. You can't swap のに in where there's no element of "and yet!"

と vs や — complete vs partial list

と = exactly these and nothing else (犬と猫 = a dog and a cat, full stop). や = these as examples, more exist (犬や猫 = dogs, cats, and other animals).

そう vs そう — eyes vs ears

Attached to a stem → "looks like" (what you see): 降りそう = looks like it'll rain. Attached to the plain form → "I hear that" (what you were told): 降るそう = I hear it'll rain.
