仮名 ・ かな

Hiragana and katakana are Japan's two syllabaries — parallel sets of 46 base characters covering the same sounds. Hiragana writes native words and grammar; katakana writes loanwords, foreign names, and emphasis. The grids below cover the base sounds, the voiced dakuten/handakuten rows, and the small-ya/yu/yo combos. Tap any cell to hear it, toggle romaji off to self-test, and run the timed drill at the bottom.

💡 Tip: read straight down each column — あ・い・う・う・え・お vowels stay constant while the consonant changes across rows. Watch the red-flag readings: し=shi, ち=chi, つ=tsu, ふ=fu, を=wo (≈o).

濁点・半濁点 — dakuten / handakuten

Add two dots (゛) to make a sound "heavier" — k→g, s→z, t→d, h→b — or a small circle (゜) on the h-row for the p-sounds.

Combos

拗音 — yōon (small ゃ ゅ ょ)

A consonant + i-kana followed by a small や/ゆ/よ blends into one syllable: き + ゃ → きゃ (kya).
