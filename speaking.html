Spoken Japanese is built from a small, stable set of sounds, with meaning carried by vowel length, doubled consonants, and pitch rather than stress. This page covers the sound inventory and its traps for English speakers, a short pitch-accent primer, and high-frequency phrases with audio for shadowing — playing a line and immediately saying it back aloud, matching the rhythm.

音

Pronunciation, the short version

get these and you sound clean

Japanese is built from a small, stable set of sounds — once you nail them they never drift, unlike English vowels. The traps for English speakers:

あいうえおFive pure vowels, always the same. a="ah", i="ee", u="oo" (lips relaxed, not rounded), e="eh", o="oh". Every kana ends in one of these.
ら り る れ ろThe "r" is a light tap — between English r, l, and d. Flick the tongue once behind the teeth, like the middle of "butter."
ふ"fu" is soft — no teeth on lip. Blow gently like blowing out a candle ("hu/fu").
つ"tsu" — the "ts" of "cats," but at the start of the syllable.
ー / おうLong vowels matter. おばさん (aunt) vs おばあさん (grandma) differ only in length — and in meaning. Hold long vowels for a full extra beat.
っSmall tsu = a silent pause. One beat of held breath: きって (kit-te, stamp) vs きて (ki-te, come).
です / ますThe u often goes near-silent. "desu" → "dess", "masu" → "mass". Native-sounding and easy.
は・へ・をParticle spellings lie. As particles they're said wa, e, and o — not ha, he, wo.
高

Pitch accent, gently

高低アクセント — don't stress, pitch

Japanese doesn't stress syllables louder like English — it raises and drops pitch. You don't need to master this now, but knowing it exists stops you from sounding flat. Each mora is either high or low; the classic minimal pair:

はし (箸)

HA-shi — high then low = chopsticks.

はし (橋)

ha-SHI — low then high = bridge.

🎧 How to practise: for now, just imitate. When you shadow a phrase, copy its melody, not just its sounds. Your ear will pick up the patterns long before you can name them.
