The characters

Kanji — characters that
carry meaning and sound.

漢字 ・ かんじ

Kanji are logographic characters adopted from Chinese, used alongside kana to write most nouns, verb stems, and adjectives. Everyday Japanese runs on roughly 2,100 of them, and most carry two kinds of reading — a borrowed on'yomi used in compounds and a native kun'yomi used standalone. Below: the core set with readings and audio, and a quiz that drills the character-to-sound link. Both can focus on just the kanji in a connected Anki deck.

On'yomi vs kun'yomi

the one idea that unlocks readings

Most kanji have two kinds of reading, and knowing which one applies is most of the battle. Here's the whole concept in one example — 水 (water):

訓読み KUN'YOMI — native reading
水 → みず
Used when the kanji stands alone or carries a hiragana tail (okurigana). This is the original Japanese word.
音読み ON'YOMI — borrowed reading
水曜日 → すいようび
Used in compounds (kanji glued to other kanji). Borrowed from Chinese centuries ago.

⚡ Rough rule of thumb: kanji alone or with okurigana → reach for kun. Kanji stuck to another kanji (a 熟語/jukugo compound) → reach for on. In this hub, on'yomi shows in red, kun'yomi in teal. It's not 100%, but it's right far more often than not.
