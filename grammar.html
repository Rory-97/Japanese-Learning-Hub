Sentence structure

the mental model

Verb last, particles do the work

Japanese is subject–object–verb: the verb ends the sentence, and everything before it is tagged with a particle showing its role. Because the particles carry the roles, word order before the verb is flexible.

私は毎朝コーヒーを飲む。
私は (topic: me) 毎朝 (every morning) コーヒーを (object) 飲む (drink) — "I drink coffee every morning."

は sets the topic, が marks the subject

は says "as for X…" and frames the sentence; が points at who/what does it, especially new information. Both can appear in one sentence.

象は鼻が長い。
"As for elephants (は), the nose (が) is long." The full breakdown is in the particles chapter below.

Say less: obvious things are dropped

Subjects and objects vanish whenever context makes them clear — pronouns like "I" and "it" are usually omitted, not implied by conjugation. A complete exchange:

食べた？"(Did you) eat?"
食べた。"(I) ate."

Questions & descriptions don't reorder

Questions add か (polite) or a rising の (casual) — no word-order change. Descriptions come before the noun, with no "that/which":

行きますか。"Are (you) going?"
昨日買った本"the book (I) bought yesterday" — a whole clause modifying 本

助詞 — the glue of every sentence

Particles are the joints of a sentence. Japanese word order is loose because each little hiragana tag tells you the job a word is doing — who acts, what gets acted on, where, when, and how clauses connect. Learn the tag, and the sentence falls into place. Every common particle below, grouped by job and colour, each with a memory hook and a tiny example.

D

But · because · and

接続助詞 — the clause joiners

This is where most learners get stuck, because English uses one word ("but", "because") where Japanese has several — each with a different flavour. These all join two clauses inside one sentence (they sit at the end of the first clause). Here's how to tell them apart.

"But" — が ・ けど ・ のに

が / けど — plain "but"
高いけど、買う。
It's pricey, but I'll buy it.
のに — "even though…!"
勉強したのに、落ちた。
Even though I studied, I failed.
Tell them apart: が (formal) and けど (casual) are neutral "but." のに carries surprise or complaint — the result was the opposite of what you'd expect. If you feel frustration or "and yet!", reach for のに.

"Because" — から ・ ので

から — personal, blunt
寒いから、閉めて。
It's cold, so close it.
ので — soft, polite
遅れたので、すみません。
I'm sorry, as I was late.
Tell them apart: both mean "because." から is subjective and direct — your own reasoning, can sound assertive ("because I said so"). ので is softer and more objective — use it for polite excuses and requests.
し
shi
and what's more (stacking reasons)

Hook: "…and besides." Piles up reasons or qualities.

安いし、おいしい。It's cheap, and tasty too.
て
-te / -de
and then / and so (verb glue)

Hook: the universal connector. Chains actions in sequence.

起きて、食べて、行く。Get up, eat, and go.
ながら
nagara
while doing (same person)

Hook: "meanwhile, me." Two actions at once by one person.

歌いながら歩く。Walk while singing.
たり
-tari
doing things like A and B

Hook: a sample list of actions — "things such as…"

食べたり飲んだりする。Eat and drink (among other things).

The "if / when" joiners — と ・ ば ・ たら ・ なら

と — automatic result
押すと開く。
If/when you push it, it opens.
ば — general hypothetical
安ければ買う。
If it's cheap, I'll buy it.
たら — once A, then B
着いたら電話して。
Call me once you arrive.
なら — if it's the case that
行くなら京都へ。
If you're going, go to Kyoto.
Quick sort: と = unavoidable/natural consequence. ば = textbook "if". たら = the all-purpose spoken "if/when" (when in doubt, use this). なら = "if that's the situation you're describing." Full formation rules in the grammar points below.

F

Like & seems

みたい ・ よう ・ らしい ・ っぽい ・ そう

English "like" and "seems" cover comparison and guessing, so these five overlap and get confused. The trick is what each is based on — your eyes, your ears, or a resemblance — and how formal it is.

みたい
mitai
like / seems (casual)

Hook: the everyday "like." Resemblance or a casual guess.

子供みたい。(They're) like a child.
よう
yō (da / ni)
like / as if (formal twin of みたい)

Hook: formal みたい. Also ように = "so that / in such a way."

夢のようだ。It's like a dream.
らしい
rashii
apparently / so very ___

Hook: two jobs — "by all accounts" (hearsay), OR "the very essence of" a noun.

男らしい。Manly / very much a man.
っぽい
-ppoi
-ish / prone to

Hook: literally English "-ish," often a touch judgy.

子供っぽい。Childish.
そう
sō
looks like… / I hear that… (two meanings!)

The big trap: そう means two different things depending on what it attaches to — your eyes vs your ears.

Stem + そう = your eyes
おいしそう。
It looks tasty. (appearance)
Plain form + そう = your ears
おいしいそう。
I hear it's tasty. (hearsay)

Memory line: chop the word short (stem) → it's what you see. Leave it whole (plain form) → it's what you were told.

G

Sentence starters

接続詞 — conjunctions

Technically these aren't particles — they're conjunctions that begin a new sentence rather than joining clauses inside one. But since they're the standalone versions of "but / so / and", they belong on the same map.

でも / しかし
But / However

でも casual, しかし formal. Start of a contrasting sentence.

疲れた。でも行く。I'm tired. But I'll go.
だから / それで
So / Therefore

だから = "that's why"; それで = "and so then."

雨だ。だから休む。It's raining. So I'll rest.
なぜなら
Because (formal)

Sets up a reason, usually closed by …から. Written/formal.

なぜなら…から。The reason is… because…
そして / それから
And then

Sequencing two sentences — "and then next…"

食べた。そして寝た。I ate. And then I slept.
それに / さらに
Moreover / Furthermore

Adds another point on top — "what's more."

安い。それに近い。It's cheap. Plus it's close.
つまり / たとえば
In other words / For example

つまり restates; たとえば illustrates.

たとえば、寿司。For example, sushi.

Confusion-busters

the pairs everyone mixes up

If you only memorise one section, make it this one. These six contrasts cause the most mistakes.

は vs が — topic vs subject

は — known / the theme
私は学生です。
As for me, I'm a student. (here's the topic)
が — new / picks one out
誰が学生？
Who is the student? (which one?)
Fast test: question words (誰, 何, どれ) always take が, and so does the answer to them. Old, already-known info that sets the scene takes は. Inside relative clauses, use が.

に vs で — being vs doing

に — where it exists
公園にいる。
I'm at the park. (just existing there)
で — where action happens
公園で遊ぶ。
I play at the park. (an action occurs)
Rule of thumb: if a verb of doing something is involved, the location takes で. If it's about existing / arriving / being placed, use に.

から vs ので — blunt vs polite "because"

から = your personal reasoning, can sound forceful. ので = softer, more objective, the polite choice for excuses and requests. Same meaning, different temperature.

けど vs のに — neutral vs frustrated "but"

けど = plain contrast, no emotion. のに = the result defied expectations; carries surprise, regret, or complaint. You can't swap のに in where there's no element of "and yet!"

と vs や — complete vs partial list

と = exactly these and nothing else (犬と猫 = a dog and a cat, full stop). や = these as examples, more exist (犬や猫 = dogs, cats, and other animals).

そう vs そう — eyes vs ears

Attached to a stem → "looks like" (what you see): 降りそう = looks like it'll rain. Attached to the plain form → "I hear that" (what you were told): 降るそう = I hear it'll rain.

Verb conjugation

動詞 — dōshi

Three groups. Group 2 (る-verbs): drop る, add ending. Group 1 (う-verbs): change the final u-sound. Plus two irregulars, する and 来る.

FormGroup 2 — 食べるGroup 1 — 飲むする来る
dictionary食べる飲むする来る
polite (〜ます)食べます飲みますします来ます
negative (〜ない)食べない飲まないしない来ない
te-form食べて飲んでして来て
past (〜た)食べた飲んだした来た
potential (can)食べられる飲めるできる来られる
volitional (let's)食べよう飲もうしよう来よう

The te-form rule (Group 1 endings)

う・つ・る→ って (買う→買って)ぬ・ぶ・む→ んで (飲む→飲んで)
く→ いて (書く→書いて)ぐ→ いで (泳ぐ→泳いで)
す→ して (話す→話して)行く→ 行って (exception!)

Politeness & register

two gears: です・ます and plain

Every sentence runs in one of two registers. Polite (です・ます) is for strangers, customers, teachers, and work. Plain form is for friends, family, inner thoughts — and it's also the form used inside grammar (before から, と思う, relative clauses…), so it's not optional. Textbooks start polite; anime and manga are overwhelmingly plain and casual, which is why mined sentences often look different from textbook ones.

The same sentence in both registers

PolitePlain
食べます食べる
食べません食べない
食べました食べた
学生ですか。学生なの？

Casual contractions you'll hear constantly

〜てる= 〜ている (食べてる = is eating)
〜とく= 〜ておく (買っとく = I'll buy it in advance)
〜ちゃう= 〜てしまう (忘れちゃった = I went and forgot)
〜ちゃ= 〜ては (行っちゃだめ = you can't go)
〜なきゃ= 〜なければ (行かなきゃ = gotta go)
〜って= 〜という／と (雨だって = they say it'll rain)
〜んだ・〜の= のだ explanatory (何してんの？ = whatcha doing?)

🎧 Mining tip: when a line from anime doesn't match any conjugation table, it's usually one of these contractions. Un-contract it (てる→ている, ちゃった→てしまった) and the grammar becomes searchable.
